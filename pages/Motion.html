Motion

Motion is the change in position of an object over time. It can be described in terms of displacement, distance, speed, velocity, and acceleration. Understanding motion is fundamental to the study of physics as it helps explain how objects interact with forces and energy.

Key Concepts in Motion

  • Displacement: A vector quantity that refers to the change in position of an object. It has both magnitude and direction.
  • Distance: A scalar quantity that refers to how much ground an object has covered during its motion.
  • Speed: A scalar quantity that refers to how fast an object is moving. It is calculated as distance divided by time (s = d/t).
  • Velocity: A vector quantity that refers to the rate at which an object changes its position. It is calculated as displacement divided by time (v = Δx/Δt).
  • Acceleration: A vector quantity that refers to the rate at which an object's velocity changes over time. It is calculated as change in velocity divided by time (a = Δv/Δt).

Interpreting displacement-time, velocity-time and acceleration-time graphs

As you can see, when displacement is increasing at a constant rate, where displacement is directly proportional to time, the velocity is constant.

This shows that obtaining the velocity graph from a displacement graph is just diffrentiating the function used to graph displacement

Just in case it is not clear yet,
lets suppose this is the equation for displacement: \[ s = 10t \] \[ \frac {ds} {dt} = \frac {ds} {dt} \cdot (10t) \] \[ \frac {ds} {dt} \cdot (10t) = 10 \] \[ \frac {ds} {dt} = v \]

Thus:

\[ v = 10 \]

Equations of Motion

The equations of motion describe the relationship between displacement, velocity, acceleration, and time for uniformly accelerated motion. The three main equations are:

  • v = u + at
  • s = 0.5(v+u)t
  • s = ut + 0.5at²
  • v² = u² + 2as

Where:

  • v = final velocity (m/s)
  • u = initial velocity (m/s)
  • a = acceleration (m/s²)
  • s = displacement (m)
  • t = time (s)

Derivation of Equations of Motion

They can be derived from the definitions of velocity and acceleration, along with the assumption of constant acceleration. For example, the first equation (v = u + at) comes directly from the definition of acceleration as the change in velocity over time. The second and third equations can be derived by integrating the acceleration and velocity equations.

Another - more mathematical - way to derive these equations is to use integration:

\[ \int_{}^{} ( v ) dt = \int_{}^{} ( u + at ) dt \]

First, we integrate both sides of the original velocity - time graph equation with respect to 't' and use the POWER RULE

Remember to treat any other variable except 't' as a constant

\[ \frac{vt^{0+1}}{0+1} = \frac{ut^{0+1}}{0+1} + \frac{at^{1+1}}{1+1} \] \[ vt = ut + 0.5 a t^2 \]

However, we still dont have our 's' component equal to our equation...

\[ v = \frac {s}{t} \] \[ s = v t \]

Thus:

\[ vt = ut + 0.5 a t^2 \]

Can be written as:

\[ s = ut + 0.5 a t^2 \]

The last thing you need to know are the 7 S.I units. These are base units which are what every other form of measurement are compared to. They serve as the modern form of the metric system, used world wide.
They are:

  • Meter (\(m\)): For length
  • Kilogram (\(kg\)): for mass
  • Second (\(s\)): For time
  • Ampere (\(A\)): for electric current
  • Kelvin (\(K\)): for temperature
  • Mole (\(mol\))
  • Candela (\(cd\)): for light intensity


Here are some examples of derived units:

  • Newton (\(N\)): the unit of force, equivalent to \(kg\cdot m/s^{2}\)
  • Joule (\(J\)): the unit of work or energy, equivalent to \(N\cdot m\)
  • Pascal (\(Pa\)): the unit of pressure, equivalent to \(N/m^{2}\)
  • Watt (\(W\)): the unit of power, equivalent to \(J/s\)
  • Meters per second (\(m/s\)): the unit for velocity and speed
  • Meters per second squared (\(m/s^{2}\)): the unit for acceleration
  • Cubic meters (\(m^{3}\)): the unit for volume
  • Square meters (\(m^{2}\)): the unit for area

Projectile motion

To understand projectile motion, you first need to understand the nature of vector quantities and how they can be broken up into vertical and horizontal components

As a ball is moving at an angle, it is moving forward a certain velocity and upwards a certain velocity. We can calculate these velocity components using our SohCahToa rules as \(V_{y}\) and \(V_{x}\) are perpendicular.
Thus, we can write \(V_{y}\) and \(V_{x}\) as: \[ V_{y} = V \cdot sin(\theta) \] \[ V_{x} = V \cdot cos(\theta) \] where \(\theta\) is the angle the ball is thrown at

Now lets do a question (remember, this concept applies to all vector quantities, such as force, momentum etc.)

First, let us find the weight of the trolley...

we know that: \[ W = m \cdot g \] Thus \[ W = 0.2 \cdot 9.81 \newline W = 1.962 N \newline \newline V = W \newline V = [1.962 N] \] Now let us use the formula from before (remember, we are trying to find the component of the weight along the slope, which is the vertical component in the diagram): \[ V_{y} = V \cdot sin(\theta) \newline \theta = 1.8^{\circ} \newline v_{y} = 1.962 \cdot sin(1.8)^{\circ} \newline v_{y} = 0.0616_{3.s.f} \newline \newline [0.0616 N \approx 0.06 N] \]

Now lets look at a projectile motion question:

In this question, we are given the height the ball was thrown from, the distance between the cannon and the ship, and the initial vertical velocity (which is 0 as it is shot at horizontally)
Out of the 2 distances given, the one we will use is the vertical one, we choose this one as acceleration due to gravity (9.81 m/s^2) acts downwards, which causes the ball to touch the floor. Now, we can use the SUVAT equations we derived previously (to choose which equation to use, you compare what values you are given and what you have to find with the equations provided on the sheet, then choose accordingly).
Thus: \[ s_{y}=u_{y}t + \frac{1}{2} a_{y} t^{2} \newline \newline s_{y} = 67 \newline u_{y} = 0 \newline a_{y} = 9.81 \newline \newline 67 = 0 \cdot t + \frac{1}{2} \cdot 9.81 t^{2} \newline \newline 67 = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 9.81 t^{2} \newline \newline rearrange \newline \newline t^{2} = \frac{2 \cdot 67}{9.81} \newline t^{2} = 13.66_{2.d.p} \newline t = 3.696 s \newline \newline [3.696 s \approx 3.7 s] \]

For this question, we need to use the t value we calculater previously. As we know the distance to the ship and the time it takes to reach it, we can use the v=s/t equation to work out its initial horizontal velocity (constant) \[ V_{x} = \frac{S_{x}}{t} \newline \newline V_{x} = \frac{150}{3.696} \newline \newline V_{x} = [40.6ms^{-1}]_{3.s.f} \]

To do this question, we use our answer from part (ii.) and (iii.) and apply our knowledge of vector components.

First we need to find the magnitude of the force, which is simple as we use pythagoras' theorem: \[ V^{2} = v_{y}^{2} + v_{x}^{2} \] as the question explicitly states just before it hits the ship, our \(v_{y}\) and \(v_{x}\) are: \[ v_{y} = 36.3 \newline \newline v_{x} = 40.6 \newline \newline Thus: \newline \newline V = \sqrt{36.3^{2} + 40.6^{2}} \newline \newline V = [54.5 ms^{-1}]_{3.s.f} \] Now, we need find the direction, which is the angle at which it is travelling during that time.
To calculate this, we will use SohCahToa \[ tan(\theta) = \frac{OPP}{ADJ} \newline \newline tan(\theta) = \frac{v_{y}}{v_{x}} \newline \newline tan(\theta) = \frac{36.3}{40.6} \newline \newline \theta = arctan(\frac{36.3}{40.6}) \newline \newline \theta = [41.8^{\circ}]_{3.s.f} \] As you can see, projectile motion is nothing more than applying your knowledge on SUVAT equations and vectors, there is nothing new.
